ABOUT THE WORK

When I found printmaking I was not prepared for the effect it would have on my practice. I took a class at UCLA in intaglio printmaking, which is the process of applying a ground to a copper plate, drawing your image using an etching tool, and the area you carved away is what will then be etched into the plate when applied to acid. The drawing itself will become a divet in the plate, providing a space for the ink to sit in before printing. The lovely thing about this technique is that everything is reversed once printed. Think of a stamp.

Depedning on the ground used, the print will have more or less textural qualities. Softground is sensitive, making any small touch on the plate cause a mark which will then be etched and visible in the final print. My favorite thing about this medium is it's unpredicatablity. You may spend hours drawing your image, and once printed it presents completely differently.

This has assisted me spectacularly because I have a tendency to need control. This obsession with perfection restricts my work, at times can make it work for me, but the result I prefer does not align with this process. Printmaking has given me the freedom to experiment and no matter how great the image itself is, the additonal textures and effects caused by the process alone leave me greatly satisfied.

My work is always in flux. I don't like to hold myself to any specific exploration. Whatever interests me, monthly, daily, hourly, is what guides my process. I am fascinated with form, mundanity, everyday expereinces, and childhood. There will always be more, or less, incorporated, but I prefer to let the viewer decide for themselves what the work is evoking. Since I already tend to overthink, I like my practice to be fluid and spontaneous. Hopefully that translates, while technique is absolutely valued along the way.
